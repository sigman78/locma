The net got spectrally simpler as it closed the search gap

E36 trained a lookahead-free reactive net by prioritized fictitious self-play (PFSP), warm-started from e29slim. Across eight generations the fair play-time searcher rbeam:shared lost its entire edge over the pure net — from winning 75–81% of games down to a coin flip (~0.51, CI straddles 0.50), replicated on a second box (M1, fresh seed). This page asks the weight-space question: what changed inside the net? The answer is unexpectedly clean — every layer's weight matrix lost effective rank, monotonically, generation by generation. Self-play didn't add capacity to beat search; it concentrated what was there. The same dynamic E29 found when a 7.4×-smaller extractor won.

Two curves, one direction

Left: the behaviour — the searcher's win rate over the pure net, per generation (x86 primary chain where measured, M1 confirmation chain full). Down = the gap closing. Right: the mechanism — mean effective rank of the net's weight matrices, x86 chain. Both fall together; avg-hard3 (the skill ruler) stays pinned at ceiling, so this is not the net getting worse.
