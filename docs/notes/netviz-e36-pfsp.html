E36 PFSP: the first training regime that dented the search gap

Every prior lever changed the net or its loss — a bigger trunk (E28/E29), a denser reward (E34), an imitation target (E9/E15). All of them either won small or closed. E36 changes none of that. It keeps the exact e29slim arch and asks a different question: what if the opponent is the lever? Following ByteRL (arXiv 2303.04096), the net best-responds to a growing population of frozen past selves, resampled per game and prioritised toward the matchups it is losing. Two generations, ~3M steps. The pure net went up on both rulers — and for the first time fair play-time search lost ground against it.

The loop — prioritized fictitious self-play

One generation is a full best-response cycle. The net warm-starts from the previous champion, trains against a mixture that samples a frozen pool member per game, then the pool is re-weighted toward whatever beat it. The new net is admitted as a member; the loop repeats. Nothing in the net changes — the opponent distribution is what moves.

The pool — who the net fights

Any registry spec can be a pool member. The seed pool mixes a frozen strong self with the scripted zoo and the known boardkeep exploiter — so self-play never forgets the guard hole it already patched. Weights are re-set each generation to max(0.1, 1−WR): the more the net wins, the less that opponent is drawn.

Why this lever, now

The reactive-limits program has swept the obvious axes. Three whole classes closed or saturated. The training regime — who you play and how the schedule adapts — was the one class never varied: every net so far learned against the same fixed scripted zoo.

The ByteRL arch comparison settled first that their net is simpler than ours (flat head + mean-pool + LSTM, no pointer). So the thing that let a pure policy beat search in our game can’t be their architecture — it must be the self-play regime. That is exactly what E36 isolates.

The headline — fair play-time search lost ground

The Phase-3 wall: even the best pure net gets beaten by fair, matched-draft search (rbeam:shared) roughly 4 games in 5. Lower bars mean the pure net is harder for search to beat. E36 gen1 pulled that number down by 0.104 with disjoint CIs — and rbeam:shared was never in gen1’s pool, so this is generalisation to a held-out strong searcher, not overfitting to a training opponent.

Methodology validated: the e29slim control here reads 0.807, reproducing Phase-3’s independently-measured 0.808 almost exactly. Same ruler, moved by 0.104. This is the first training-side dent in the search gap that Phase 3 established as the reactive net’s wall.

Second ruler — avg-hard3

Win rate vs the three hardest scripted opponents, paired against e29slim on identical held-out states (n=150/opp, seed 40M). The pool is near-saturated here, so this is the softer read — but it moves the right way and confirms nothing regressed.

Boardkeep exploit not opened — gen1 wins 0.767 of that matchup (driver eval; the proper E10 bench is a follow-up).

Fictitious play is working

Internal head-to-head win rates confirm the generational ladder is monotone — each net beats the one before it, and both beat the frozen starting champion. That is the signature of a healthy best-response loop, not a random walk.

What this is — and isn’t — yet

Gate 0 is a signal test, not a promotion. The scale is a rounding error next to the paper that inspired it: 2 generations, ~3M steps, one seed, ~3 hours on one GPU vs ByteRL’s cluster-weeks of population play. The point was to find out whether the regime moves the needle at all before spending the budget. It does.

Caveats. Single seed, 2 generations — the gain may grow with scale or plateau. avg-hard3 is pool-saturated (trust the search dent). Boardkeep is a driver-eval number, not the E10 bench. gen1 is not a promoted artifact — that needs 3 seeds, more generations, guardrails, and fresh-anchor confirms.

Why it’s worth scaling. This is the first lever to move both rulers the right way after arch, reward, and imitation all closed — and the search dent generalised to a held-out searcher. The loop is still monotone at 3M steps, so there is headroom to buy.

Next. Scale the PFSP program — more generations and budget — then the arch-retest arm: every “capacity/recurrence doesn’t help” verdict (E6 LSTM, E29 slim) was measured under the weak fixed-zoo signal. Under the richer self-play signal, recurrence especially should be re-tested rather than assumed dead. The driver isolates the regime lever first; the arch retest rides on top of it.

Method & provenance

Regime. Prioritized fictitious self-play, after ByteRL / optimistic smooth fictitious play (arXiv 2303.04096). The reactive net best-responds to a mixture that samples one frozen pool member per game, weighted max(0.1, 1−WR) toward losing matchups; the pool grows with past-self checkpoints (cap 4, evict oldest) plus scripted / boardkeep anchors.

Net. Unchanged from e29slim — E29 slim extractor + E28 pointer head + ldraft learned draft, token-fx observation. The only variable is the training opponent. No arch, loss, or reward edit.

Tooling. locma/policies/pfsp.py (PFSPBattleMixture), pfsp:pool.json[,draft] registry spec (hidden), generational driver scripts/e36_pfsp.py (warm-start → best-respond → eval → reweight → admit → rewrite pool). CI-safe scripted-pool test in tests/test_pfsp.py.

Gate evals. avg-hard3 = mean win rate vs the three hardest scripted opponents, paired vs e29slim on identical held-out states (n=150/opp, seed 40M). Search gap = rbeam:shared win rate against the pure net (n=400, seed 30M); rbeam:shared is not a pool member, so the dent is held-out. CIs are bootstrap; the search-gap CIs are disjoint.

Numbers. runs/e36/gate0_summary.json, driver log runs/e36/history.json, artifacts runs/e36_gen{0,1}.zip (gitignored). Control 0.807 reproduces the Phase-3 measurement of 0.808.

Provenance. Branch feat/e36-pfsp, PR #101, git 455c0ca. Prior context: E29 slim (PR #93, the arch this warm-starts from), the Phase-3 search-gap program (rbeam / netdmcts as fair search of record), E34 reward shaping (PR #100, the last closed lever). Gate 0 only — not a promoted recipe of record.
